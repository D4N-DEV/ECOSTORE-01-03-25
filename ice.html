What is Ice Watch? What is its goal?


"Ice Watch" is an art installation by Danish-Icelandic artist Olafur Eliasson and geologist Minik Rosing. It features large blocks of glacial ice harvested from the waters around Greenland and placed in public spaces to visually and physically confront people with the reality of climate change. The installation aims to create a direct, sensory experience of climate change. Instead of statistics or abstract warnings, "Ice Watch" brings melting ice into urban environments, allowing people to touch, see, and hear it as it slowly disappears. This tangible experience makes climate change more immediate and personal.


Major installations


1. Copenhagen (2014) – Launched during the UN Climate Summit, where the ice blocks were arranged in a clock-like formation outside City Hall. 2. Paris (2015) – Coincided with the COP21 climate conference, reinforcing the urgency of the Paris Agreement discussions. 3. London (2018) – 24 ice blocks were placed outside Tate Modern and in front of Bloomberg’s headquarters to highlight the consequences of global warming.


Reception


"Ice Watch" has been praised for making climate change visceral and accessible. However, some critics question the carbon footprint of transporting the ice. Despite this, Eliasson and Rosing argue that the awareness it generates far outweighs its environmental impact.
